### State of Deployments (February 2026)

**Total global humanoid installations in 2025: ~16,000 units.** That is an incredibly small number. For context, there are ~4 million industrial robots deployed worldwide. Humanoids represent 0.4% of the robotics market.

| Metric | Number | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Total humanoid installations (2025) | ~16,000 | Mostly China (80%+) |
| Market leader by volume | AGIBOT (30.4% share, ~5,168 units) | Shanghai-based |
| #2 by volume | Unitree (26.4% share, ~5,500 units) | Hangzhou-based |
| Primary use case | Data collection, research, pilot demos | NOT commercial deployment |
| Projected 2027 installations | 100,000+ units | 6x growth in 2 years |

**Critical reality check:** Most of these 16,000 units are being used for data collection and research, not commercial deployment. Real "useful work" deployments are still single-digit pilots globally (Figure 02 at BMW, Agility Digit at Toyota Canada, a handful of others).

### Major Trends Shaping the Market

1. **China dominance:** Chinese companies account for 80-90% of global humanoid shipments. AGIBOT, Unitree, and ~140 other Chinese manufacturers are driving volume and crashing prices.

2. **Price collapse:** Unitree R1 at $5,900 (July 2025) and Noetix Bumi at $1,370 shattered assumptions about humanoid pricing. Entry-level full-size humanoids projected below $10,000-$15,000 by 2030.

3. **Software platform wars:** OpenMind App Store (Jan 2026), Unitree App Store (Dec 2025), and AGIBOT's rental platform are all fighting to become the "Android of robots." The skill/software layer is being claimed NOW.

4. **Sim-to-real maturation:** NVIDIA Isaac GR00T N1 (March 2025), Google DeepMind/NVIDIA Newton physics engine, and foundation models are making simulation-based training increasingly viable.

5. **Hardware-funded, software-starved:** Hardware companies have raised billions (Figure AI: $1.9B, Apptronik: ~$1B) but all acknowledge that software/skills are the real bottleneck.

6. **Commercial pilots, not mass deployment:** Even the most advanced deployments (Figure 02 at BMW: 1,250 hours, 90,000 parts) are tightly scoped pilots, not factory-wide rollouts.

- **Insurance:** Emerging; some insurers developing API-based underwriting pulling real-time robot performance data. No standard product yet.
- **State-level variation:** California, New York likely to regulate first. No current humanoid-specific legislation at state level.

#### EU AI Act and Robot Regulation

The EU AI Act entered into force August 1, 2024, with phased implementation:

| Date | What Applies |
|---|---|
| Feb 2, 2025 | Prohibited AI practices + AI literacy obligations |
| Aug 2, 2025 | Rules for general-purpose AI models |
| **Aug 2, 2026** | **Rules for high-risk AI systems** |
| Aug 2, 2027 | High-risk AI in regulated products (extended transition) |

**Impact on humanoid robots in hospitality:**
- A humanoid robot interacting with hotel guests will likely be classified as a **high-risk AI system** due to human interaction and safety implications
- Requires conformity assessments, risk management systems, human oversight mechanisms, transparency obligations
- The EU Machinery Regulation (replacing the Machinery Directive) adds safety requirements for robots as products
- **Timeline impact:** Any company wanting to deploy humanoids in EU hotels after August 2026 needs to demonstrate compliance. This creates both a barrier to entry AND an opportunity for compliant skill platforms.

**However:** Labor shortages are acute, especially post-pandemic. Hotels in UK and Germany are operating 20-30% understaffed. The pragmatic need may override cultural resistance.

## 7. REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

### 7A. EU AI Act (Most Comprehensive Globally)

| Phase | Date | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| Prohibited practices | Feb 2, 2025 | Social scoring, manipulative AI, real-time biometric surveillance |
| GPAI rules | Aug 2, 2025 | General-purpose AI model obligations |
| **High-risk AI** | **Aug 2, 2026** | **Conformity assessments, risk management, human oversight for high-risk AI** |
| Regulated products | Aug 2, 2027 | High-risk AI embedded in regulated products (machinery, etc.) |

**Impact on humanoid robots:**
- Humanoids in hotels will likely be classified as high-risk AI (interacting with public, potential safety risks)
- Requires: conformity assessment, risk management system, data governance, transparency, human oversight, accuracy/robustness documentation
- EU Machinery Regulation (replacing Machinery Directive) adds product safety requirements
- **Compliance cost:** Significant for small companies. Estimated EUR 50-300K for initial conformity assessment.
- **Moat opportunity:** If HomoSkill builds EU AI Act compliance into its platform, it becomes a competitive advantage in Europe

### 7B. United States (Fragmented)

| Level | Approach |
|---|---|
| Federal (OSHA) | No specific humanoid standards. General Duty Clause applies. |

| Liability | State-level product liability and tort law. No clear framework for multi-party robot incidents. |
| Insurance | Emerging. No standard humanoid liability insurance product. |

**Key concern:** The US liability landscape is the most litigious in the world. A single robot-injury lawsuit in a hotel could cost millions and set precedent for the entire industry. This risk makes US deployment more cautious despite strong ROI fundamentals.

## 9. CRITICAL GAPS & RISKS

### 9A. Battery Technology Limitations

**This is the single biggest technical barrier to humanoid deployment in hospitality.**

| Metric | Current State | Required for Hospitality |
|---|---|---|
| Operating time per charge | 1.5-2 hours | 8+ hours |
| Charge time | 50-60 minutes | <30 minutes or hot-swap |
| Cycle life (active use) | ~200 cycles | 600+ cycles |
| Years to 8-hour operation | **10+ years** (industry estimates) | — |

**Impact:** A hotel concierge that needs recharging every 90 minutes is a demo, not a service. Current solutions:
- Swap stations (requires multiple battery packs, $$$)
- Multiple robots rotating shifts (3-4 robots per station, 3-4x the cost)
- Limited-duty cycles (2 hours on, 1 hour charging = 67% uptime)

**This is why wheeled delivery bots work and humanoids don't — delivery bots return to a charging dock between deliveries.**

### 9B. Sim-to-Real Transfer Gap

The gap between simulated training and real-world performance remains one of the most critical challenges in robotics:

- Controllers trained in simulation often fail when transferred to real robots due to sensor noise, friction differences, and environmental complexity
- Modeling errors in actuator dynamics cause major inefficiencies affecting endurance and payload
- Real-world environments (hotel lobbies with varying lighting, carpet transitions, furniture rearrangements, children running) are orders of magnitude more complex than simulation
- NVIDIA Isaac Sim + GR00T N1 + Newton physics engine are improving this, but the gap is far from closed

**Impact on HomoSkill:** Any skill developed in simulation will require extensive real-world testing and refinement. The "build skills in simulation, deploy anywhere" vision is aspirational, not current reality.

### 9C. Cost Reduction Timeline

| Year | Expected Entry-Level Price | Expected BOM Cost |
|---|---|---|
| 2026 | $13,500-$20,000 (Unitree G1) | $8,000-$12,000 |
| 2027 | $10,000-$15,000 | $6,000-$9,000 |
| 2028 | $8,000-$12,000 | $4,000-$7,000 |
| 2030 | $5,000-$10,000 | $3,000-$5,000 |
| 2035 | $3,000-$7,000 | $2,000-$4,000 |

**Price reduction rate:** 20-30% per year, driven by Chinese manufacturing scale, component commoditization, and competition.

**Impact on HomoSkill:** Every price drop improves the ROI case for deployment. At $5,000 hardware cost (projected ~2030), the total cost of ownership drops enough to make robot deployment viable even in moderate-wage markets like Thailand.

### 9D. Competition from Big Tech

| Company | Threat Level | What They Could Do |
|---|---|---|
| NVIDIA | High | Already provides the simulation layer everyone uses. Could vertically integrate into skills/deployment. |
| Google (DeepMind) | High | Exclusive Apptronik partnership. Could build hospitality-specific AI models. |
| Amazon | Medium | Massive investment in warehouse robotics (Agility). Could extend to delivery/hospitality. |
| Apple | Low-Medium | Vision Pro + robotics integration. No current moves but massive resources. |
| Microsoft | Low | Azure IoT for robotics. Could enter through enterprise/hotel IT relationships. |
| Tesla | Medium | Optimus is Tesla's long-term play. If successful, they'll control hardware+software stack. |

**The big tech risk:** Any of these companies could decide to enter hospitality robotics and immediately outspend every startup combined. NVIDIA's ecosystem play is the most concerning — they're already the plumbing for the entire industry.

### 9E. Regulatory Uncertainty

| Risk | Probability | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| EU AI Act classifies hotel robots as high-risk | High | Significant compliance costs; barrier to entry |
| US state-level robot taxes | Medium | Some states considering "automation taxes" |
| Safety incident triggers regulatory crackdown | Medium | One high-profile injury could freeze deployments industry-wide |
| Data privacy restrictions on robot cameras | High | GDPR, PDPA, and similar laws restrict robot sensors in public spaces |
| Labor union opposition | Medium (high in Europe) | Political pressure to restrict automation |

### 9F. Economic Downturn Risks

| Scenario | Impact on Humanoid Robotics |
|---|---|
| Global recession | VC funding dries up; hotel capex budgets freeze; pilots cancelled |
| Regional recession (e.g., China property crisis) | Chinese manufacturers slash prices further; Southeast Asian tourism declines |
| Oil price crash | Middle East tech spending collapses; Gulf expansion plans pause |
| Interest rate spikes | RaaS (Robot-as-a-Service) financing becomes expensive; deployment payback periods extend |

**Historical precedent:** Robotics investment is highly cyclical. The previous "robot revolution" hype (2015-2017) was followed by a correction. The current cycle is much larger and more substantiated, but a recession could delay commercial adoption by 2-3 years.

### 9G. The "Good Enough" Risk

The most underappreciated risk: **wheeled delivery bots are "good enough" for most hospitality use cases.** KEENON and Pudu have deployed 150,000+ units because:
- They work reliably
- They're cheaper ($5K-$15K vs $20K-$90K for humanoids)
- Battery life is not an issue (dock between tasks)
- They don't fall over
- They don't need to navigate stairs (elevators work)

If the market decides that wheeled bots handle 80% of the need, the addressable market for humanoids in hospitality shrinks dramatically. The remaining 20% (greeting, concierge, complex physical tasks) may not justify the humanoid premium.

### 10G. The Honest Bottom Line

**For a bootstrapped solo founder in Bangkok with zero funding, zero customers, and zero deployed technology, the global market analysis points to one conclusion:**

Do not try to build a global platform. Build a local services business.

**What works globally for HomoSkill:**
1. Hospitality-specific deployment expertise (consulting, integration, training)
2. Local market knowledge in Thailand/SEA that Chinese and American companies lack
3. Building relationships with hardware companies who need local partners
4. Creating certification programs for the emerging robot workforce
5. Eventually productizing consulting knowledge into software tools

**What does NOT work globally for HomoSkill:**
1. Competing with OpenMind as a horizontal platform ($0 vs $20M)
2. Competing with KEENON/Pudu on hardware+deployment ($0 vs $100M+ in deployments)
3. Selling to US/EU markets without local presence, credibility, or compliance
4. Building 4 products simultaneously (Sapien Core, Studio, Fleet, Sense)
5. Claiming "hardware-agnostic" while supporting only one platform

**The global market is enormous ($30-50B by 2035). HomoSkill's slice of it will be defined not by the size of the market but by the founder's ability to execute locally first, then expand through partnerships.** Every major global player (AGIBOT, KEENON, OpenMind) needs local partners in every market. That is the realistic global opportunity.
